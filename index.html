Abstract

This study delves into the prevalent problem concerning smoking cigarettes, which has been ingrained into peoples lives since the late 19th century. Centering on maternal smoking and complications during pregnancy, research links smoking to the low birth weights and the development of cardiovascular diseases like myocardial infarction and conotruncal heart defects. Additionally, it aims to highlight substances contained in cigarettes such as nicotine, tar and carbon monoxide and its detrimental impacts on health. In exploring the risks of certain carcinogens, such as benzene and nitrosamines, it can be said that the risk becomes more hazardous depending on the magnitude. Despite all this, statistics show that 9.5% of women were smoking at time of delivery so health experts provide safer alternatives like nicotine intervention therapy, which helps pregnant women with cravings. This article raises awareness of the dangers of smoking that pregnant women may be unaware of.

Introduction

Cigarettes have been around since the late 1800’s and have been implemented as a necessity into many people’s daily lives. Around 1.3 billion of the population smoke cigarettes worldwide. However, the implications of smoking have been a main topic of conversation throughout the years, especially their effect on pregnancy. The intake of certain carcinogens has been linked to the development of many cardiovascular diseases while also leading to complications on infancy and health in the long term.

Analysis and discussion

Carcinogens in cigarettes and their effect on health

Cigarette smoking has contributed to the development of health problems and premature deaths and is considered a major risk for the development of cancer, cardiovascular diseases and fetal developmental abnormalities (CDC, 2020) due to the carcinogens in them. Carcinogens are substances that tend to cause cancer (Hutter, 2023) They directly harm the body, and the impact increases the more the average person smokes. Exposure to carcinogens such as nitrosamines is due to the continual intake of tobacco. The levels of tobacco specific nitrosamines (NNN and NNK) depend on how it is prepared (Yalcin and de la Monte, 2016). Higher levels are found in certain types and strains of tobacco leaf (RIVM, 2018). Tobacco-specific nitrosamines are broken down by enzymes causing damage to DNA and (RIVM, 2018) tissue injury causing malignant transformations. Without treatment, this can lead to the development of cancer predominantly in the lungs or airways and it is shown to lead to cancer in foetus. Another potent carcinogen in cigarettes is benzene. Benzene causes harmful effects on bone marrow and a decrease in red blood cells (CDC, 2018). The department of Health and Human Services has determined that benzene is a factor that contributes to the development of cancer(CDC, 2018). Some women who breathed high levels of benzene were shown to have irregular periods however it is unknown whether this exposure affects their fertility (CDC, 2018).

Complications in pregnancy

Pregnant woman who smoke are at increased risk of development of myocardial infarction (heart attack) as it is one of the causes (Nanda, Nelson-Piercy and Mackillop, 2012) of premature death and is detrimental to both mother and baby. Female smokers are reported to have 2-fold increased risk and also doing this while taking oral contraceptives increases risk by 7 to 100-fold. (James et al., 2006) Smoking damages the heart and blood vessels with substances such as nicotine, carbon monoxide and tar all contributing to this; they act by replacing the oxygen in your blood and coating your lungs like soot in a chimney, which cuts off oxygen supply making it hard to breathe (Department of Health, 2022). Low blood supply to the embryo causes a decrease in mental development also affecting the growth of new cells in the baby (Moore and Persaud, p.185, 1998). Maternal smoking is also known to be one of the main causes of growth retardation (Moore and Persaud, p.185, 1998). This is when a baby in the womb does not grow as expected and does not follow the progression expected at the stage of the mother’s pregnancy (Hirsch, 2020). This leads to the baby being physically underdeveloped. Aswell as that, low birth weight is the chief reason for premature infant death and directly correlates to maternal and paternal smoking, as well as the chance of obtaining conotruncal heart defects and limb deficiencies (Moore and Persaud, p.185, 1998) . Conotruncal heart defects are a group of cardiovascular anomalies involving the outflow tracts and great vessels (Weerakkody, 2022) . A study (Hutt et al., 2005) compares female mice’s body weights with and without exposure to cigarette smoke. The mice were exposed in whole body inhalation exposure chambers for 30 months; 5 days a week from the age of 6 weeks. The results, shown in Figure 1 show a significantly less mean body weight for the mice exposed to the cigarette smoke compared to the mice that had no exposure. This can be used to prove the correlation of smoking and low birth weights in pregnancy.

Despite these facts, on average statistics show that in the years 2021/2022, 9.5% of women were smoking at the time of delivery (Action on Smoking and Health, 2021) . Even if the mother doesn’t smoke, second hand smoking is still able to occur. For example, paternal smoking can still have an effect as the mother is still inhaling harmful chemicals which is then passed onto the baby.

Quitting smoking for 9 months may be quite challenging but it benefits both mother and child. Nicotine replacement therapy (NRT) is an alternative to help quit smoking during pregnancy. NRT is much safer as it does not include carbon monoxide or tar (Ash, 2021). NRT can help you successfully stop smoking in pregnancy and reduce the risk of miscarriage, stillbirth, poor growth and development and much more (Ash, 2021). NRT comes in different forms such as patches, nicotine chewing gum and inhalators which helps reduce unpleasant withdrawal effects such as bad moods and cravings. It will help with quitting but if used for long term independence, you should consult your health advisor (American Cancer Society, 2021).

Conclusion

In conclusion, the alarming health impacts of maternal smoking should be made more aware as it can lead to many unforeseen complications to the mother and their offspring. Health experts strongly discourage smoking during pregnancy and instead they propose an alternative of nicotine replacement therapy (NRT) which helps women decrease the use of cigarettes during maternity which can eventually lead to quitting altogether. Given the complexities of pregnancy, the act of smoking during this crucial period should be minimized for the benefit of both mother and child.
